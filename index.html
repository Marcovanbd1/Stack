Premise

The question that precedes every other question

Every major discussion about neighbourhood health — in policy, in procurement, in clinical leadership — eventually circles back to the same set of problems: access, demand, fragmentation, inequality of outcome. The responses are usually structural: more staff, new pathways, better data, integrated teams.

"What needs to be true before anything else can work?"

That question is not rhetorical. It has a specific answer. And the answer is not a product, a pathway, or a policy. It is a sequence of conditions, each of which enables the next.

What follows is a description of that sequence as we have built it in practice — on a coastal peninsula in Dorset, serving 77,000 patients across five GP practices, inside one of 43 places nationally selected for the National Neighbourhood Health Implementation Programme.

NHF
The Neighbourhood Health Framework, published 17 March 2026, describes in policy language what we had already constructed. That is not a claim to primacy. It is a statement about the nature of the work: it emerged from practice, not from doctrine.
The problem nobody designed for

What happens after the appointment ends

The NHS has invested heavily in clinical AI at the point of diagnosis. Almost nobody has asked what happens after the appointment ends.

"Not absence of need. Hesitation in the presence of friction."

A patient leaves a GP consultation. They have a diagnosis, a plan, and a head full of anxiety. By the time they reach the car park, 40% of what they were told has gone. What they have in their pocket is a phone. What they get on that phone is nothing.

This is the gap. It is not a technology gap. It is a design gap. Nobody designed for what happens next.

10YHP
The 10 Year Health Plan commits to care "done with you, not to you" and personal care plans co-created with patients who have complex needs. The Stack is the infrastructure that makes those commitments technically realisable at the point of encounter — not just in the planning guidance.

The interpretive layer

Emotional Architecture

Running vertically through every layer of the Stack is a single design discipline: Emotional Architecture. It is not a therapeutic framework, a sentiment scoring tool, or a wellbeing instrument. It is a structured approach to treating the emotional climate of a human encounter as primary data.

NHF
The Neighbourhood Health Framework calls for "proactive, personalised and digitally enabled care" and "care that is co-produced with patients." Emotional Architecture is the methodology by which those commitments are operationalised rather than merely stated.

Five dimensions. Each one a distinct form of signal that a well-designed system reads before deciding what a person needs next.

I
Tone Gradient
The emotional register of an encounter as a gradient that shifts throughout. Identical words carry different meaning at different points on the gradient.
II
Disclosure Readiness
The degree to which a person is prepared to reveal their actual concern. Primary disclosure frequently differs from real concern. Final moments carry disproportionate risk.
III
Omission Topology
The structural pattern of what is not said. Which topics are avoided. Where speech becomes vague. Omission is not absence of information — it is signal in its own right.
IV
Relational Load
The power dynamic, trust relationship, and shared history shaping what is communicated explicitly versus what is carried by context.
V
Temporal Pressure
The encounter type and time context — first contact, crisis, discharge, routine review — and how these determine what is significant and what response is appropriate.
