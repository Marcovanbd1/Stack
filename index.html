The gap no current system addresses

One in five GP appointments are for non-medical reasons. Loneliness. Housing. Debt. Social isolation.

This is NHS England's own figure. It is not a clinical problem. It is a navigation problem. And it is a civic problem. People arrive at the GP because the GP is the only door they know how to find. The infrastructure that should sit between a person and their community has either never been built, or has never been connected. Every triage tool, every ambient voice platform, every patient-facing technology deployed into primary care today starts at the same moment: when a patient contacts a service.

The space before that moment, and everything after the appointment ends, is where the people with the greatest need fall through.

The NHS 10 Year Plan calls for three shifts: hospital to community, analogue to digital, sickness to prevention. The Neighbourhood Health Stack is what those shifts look like when they are built as infrastructure, from the ground up, in a real neighbourhood.

There is also a market problem. Patient activation platforms are being sold into primary care at scale. The premise is that patients need to be activated by a product. The evidence is that the people who need activation most are least likely to engage with a platform designed for people who are already engaged. The stack activates people by being the infrastructure they move through. The ones who need it most encounter it most, use it most, and learn most. That is not a feature. It is a consequence of building infrastructure that covers the whole journey.

What kind of knowledge does a neighbourhood need?

Aristotle distinguished three kinds of knowledge. Episteme: what is true. Techne: how to do something. Phronesis: the practical wisdom to know what the situation actually requires. Public services have built systems that are rich in episteme and strong on techne. The gap is almost always phronesis. The judgment that cannot be reduced to a rule. The knowledge that only comes from being present in a place, over time, with the people who live there.

The Neighbourhood Health Stack is an attempt to build infrastructure that carries all three. Navigation Intelligence is episteme: what exists, where to go. Smart Triage and Ambient Voice Technology are techne: doing the right thing, in the right way, at the right moment. Neighbourhood Signals and Support Playlists are the architecture for phronesis: the neighbourhood developing the capacity to understand itself, and act on what it knows.

Episteme
Theoretical knowledge
What exists. Where to go. L0 Navigation Intelligence. The foundation the whole stack rests on.
Techne
Practical craft
How to route equitably. How to make the encounter fully present. L1 Smart Triage. L2 Ambient Voice.
Phronesis
Practical wisdom
What the neighbourhood needs, before it knows to ask. L3 Signals. L4 Support Playlists.

A democratic system that cannot develop phronesis at neighbourhood level is not just inefficient. It is not listening. The stack is an attempt to build the conditions for listening at scale, without surveillance, without market surfaces, and without asking the people who need it most to do the work of finding it.

Five layers. One sequence. Each one unlocks the next.

The stack moves a person from the space before demand, through the clinical encounter, to navigated support on the other side. Each layer is distinct. Together they are continuous. Neighbourhood-wide deployment is what makes each layer work. A layer deployed in one practice is a tool. Deployed across the neighbourhood, it is infrastructure.

L0
Navigation Intelligence
Live, accessible, neighbourhood-wide information about what exists and where to go. Built for utility not completeness — nothing is included because it ought to be there, only because it is genuinely useful. This is the layer that addresses the space before demand. It is live in Weymouth and Portland. It is free to replicate.
Live
L1
Smart Triage
Equitable, intelligent routing to the right care, first time. Neighbourhood-wide adoption means access no longer depends on how articulate, persistent, or digitally confident you are. The equity argument is built into the layer. This is a procurement decision. The supplier becomes part of the neighbourhood health infrastructure.
Demo stage Procurement
L2
Ambient Voice Technology
The clinical encounter becomes fully present. The clinician is with the patient, not the screen. Neighbourhood-wide deployment turns every consultation into structured intelligence — for the first time at this scale. The supplier relationship at L2 determines everything that becomes possible at L4. This is the most consequential procurement decision in the stack.
Demo stage Procurement
L3
Neighbourhood Signals
The civic listening layer. Built on top of L0, it turns the navigation infrastructure into a democratic instrument. The pages people loop between, the dead ends, the drop-offs, the QR scans that cluster in specific streets: these are not analytics. They are the neighbourhood telling you where pressure is forming before it becomes demand on services. The 91-day pilot in Weymouth and Portland is complete. Three signal clusters. Zero personal data. The community is already speaking. This layer listens.
Pilot complete
L4
Support Playlists
A calm, bespoke, sequenced set of next steps. Available before an appointment, during it, or after it. Platform agnostic. Free to patients and residents at the point of use, with no market surface in primary care. The gap between the appointment and knowing what to do next, closed. The structured output from L2 makes personalised playlists possible at scale. This is what the whole stack has been building toward.

Equity and continuity

The stack does not need a patient activation platform sitting on top of it. It is the activation.

Patient activation platforms are built on an assumption: that engagement is a behaviour to be encouraged from outside. The evidence from primary care is consistent. The people with the highest need, lowest health literacy, and weakest social capital are least likely to find a platform, trust it, or persist with it. A platform that waits for engagement reproduces the inequalities it was designed to address.

The Neighbourhood Health Stack works differently. Every layer is designed to meet people where they already are. Searching for information. Contacting a service. Sitting in a consultation. Looking for what comes next. The person with the greatest health burden encounters more of the stack, more often, and derives more from each encounter. Equity and continuity are not features added to the infrastructure. They are the consequence of building infrastructure that covers the whole journey.

The human layer is not a feature of the stack. It is the methodology beneath it. A nurse, a psychologist, a social worker, and a philosopher — a century of combined experience of what happens when systems fail the people they were built to serve. That is what Neighbourhood CoLab CIC brings to the infrastructure. The space technology cannot replace, and too often forgets to leave.

The procurement map

Two layers require a supplier. The navigation foundation is built, owned, and free to replicate. The suppliers chosen for L1 and L2 become part of the neighbourhood health system. These decisions deserve the same seriousness as any other infrastructure decision.

01
Layer 1 · Access
Smart Triage
The procurement category is well-established. Clinical safety discipline is not always applied consistently across the market — for a system this embedded in the neighbourhood, it must be. Eighteen months of clinical observation precede deployment. Long-term sustainability and NHS alignment matter more here than innovation credentials.
02
Layer 2 · Encounter
Ambient Voice Technology
The frontier is not transcription accuracy — every credible platform has solved that. The question is what happens to the structured output after the consultation ends: how it reaches the patient, the care team, and the record. The supplier that owns L2 shapes what is possible at L4. That relationship should be chosen with that consequence in mind.
